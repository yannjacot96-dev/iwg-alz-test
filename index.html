🧠 About the IWG

The International Working Group brings together the world's leading experts on Alzheimer disease to develop and promote rigorous, clinically grounded diagnostic frameworks.

What is the IWG?

The International Working Group on Alzheimer Disease (IWG) is an independent, multidisciplinary consortium founded in 2004 under the leadership of Professor Bruno Dubois at the Institut de la Mémoire et de la Maladie d'Alzheimer (IM2A), Sorbonne Université, Paris. The group was established to address a growing need for internationally agreed diagnostic criteria for Alzheimer disease that reflect both the clinical and biological dimensions of the condition.

📜 A History of Landmark Criteria

The IWG has produced three generations of research criteria for Alzheimer disease: the IWG-1 criteria (2007), the IWG-2 criteria (2014), and the most recent IWG 2024 recommendations, published in JAMA Neurology. Each iteration has refined the clinical-biological definition of AD in light of new scientific evidence.

🌍 An International Consensus

The IWG unites 46 neurologists, psychiatrists, neuropsychologists, and researchers from institutions across Europe, North America, Latin America, and Australia. This breadth of expertise ensures that the group's recommendations reflect diverse clinical practices and populations worldwide.

⚖️ An Independent Voice

The IWG operates independently of pharmaceutical industry interests and regulatory bodies. Its recommendations are driven solely by scientific evidence and the imperative to protect patients from premature or inaccurate diagnoses — particularly those who are cognitively normal but biomarker-positive.

🎯 Clinical-Biological Framework

The IWG's defining position is that Alzheimer disease must remain a clinical-biological construct: a diagnosis requires both a recognised clinical phenotype (measurable cognitive symptoms) and supportive biological evidence (positive AD biomarkers). Biomarker positivity alone, in the absence of symptoms, does not constitute a diagnosis of AD.

🎯 Mission & Objectives

The IWG pursues three core objectives that guide all of its scientific and clinical activities.

🔬

Advance Scientific Knowledge

Develop and refine evidence-based diagnostic criteria for Alzheimer disease by integrating the latest advances in biomarker research, neuroimaging, and clinical phenotyping.

🏥

Guide Clinical Practice

Provide clinicians worldwide with practical, scientifically sound frameworks for diagnosing and communicating Alzheimer disease — ensuring patients receive accurate diagnoses and appropriate care.

🛡️

Protect Patients & Society

Prevent the harms of over-diagnosis and premature labelling — psychological distress, discrimination, and inappropriate treatment — by advocating for responsible use of biomarkers in cognitively normal individuals.

📄 Latest Publication

The IWG's most recent recommendations, published in JAMA Neurology in 2024.

2024 JAMA
Neurology

Alzheimer Disease is a Clinical-Biological Construct — An International Working Group Recommendation

JAMA Neurol. 2024 Dec 01; 81(12):1304–1311 · doi:10.1001/jamaneurol.2024.3770

In response to the 2024 Alzheimer Association criteria, which define AD as a purely biological entity, the IWG reaffirms that a diagnosis of Alzheimer disease requires both a recognised clinical phenotype and supportive biomarker evidence. The paper introduces a three-tier lexicon — At-Risk, Presymptomatic AD, and Alzheimer Disease — and provides four key recommendations for clinical practice, research, and public health.
